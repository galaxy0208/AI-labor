Research Extension · Agentic AI & Labor

Immigration Policy
as a Labor Buffer
in the Age of AI

Extending our analysis of agentic AI's impact on U.S. labor markets, this section examines how immigration policy — specifically the H-1B visa system and cross-national openness to foreign workers — interacts with AI-driven occupational displacement. We compare the United States, a high-immigration economy, with South Korea, a historically restrictive one.

A note on the "AI Exposure Index"

There is no single official statistic published as an "AI exposure index" on a 0–100 scale. The exposure values used throughout this site are a synthesized composite that we constructed by rescaling and combining task-level exposure estimates from Eloundou et al. (2023) (GPT task mapping) with O*NET 28.0 task descriptors, weighted by occupational employment from the BLS. It is a relative, illustrative measure for cross-occupation and cross-industry comparison — not a directly reported number, and not a probability of job loss. Read it as "higher = more of an occupation's tasks are technically exposed to current large language models." Where exposure is compared against employment or immigration data, note that those underlying series come from different years and survey methodologies; treat the comparison as directional, not precise. See the References section for every source used.

Industry

The U.S. as a High-Immigration,
High-AI-Exposure Economy

In 2025, the foreign-born accounted for 19.1% of the U.S. civilian labor force — more than 31 million workers, near a record high. This workforce is disproportionately concentrated in AI-exposed sectors: professional & business services, information technology, and healthcare. Meanwhile, the H-1B visa program channels skilled immigrants directly into the highest-exposure occupations — computer-related roles accounted for 64% of all H-1B approvals (~255,000 workers) in FY2024.

The H-1B visa effectively funnels foreign talent into the most AI-exposed occupations — software development, data analysis, financial analysis. This creates a compounding dynamic: the same roles most threatened by agentic AI are also the roles with the highest immigrant concentration. If AI displaces these jobs, immigrant workers bear disproportionate risk — while simultaneously being critical to building the AI systems causing that displacement.

Expected Outcomes

From Exposure to Employment:
Who Grows, Who Shrinks

Having established which industries carry the most AI exposure, the question becomes what happens to employment. The following figures move from the industry-level exposure picture above to occupation-level outcomes, pairing the synthesized AI Exposure Index against projected employment trends derived from BLS occupational projections (2024–2034 cycle).

The H-1B Paradox

Cheap Labor, High Exposure —
The H-1B Squeeze

Critics argue that H-1B visas allow firms to undercut domestic wages in AI-exposed roles. Proponents counter that immigrant talent builds the AI ecosystem itself. The data reveals both are partially true — creating a paradox where immigration simultaneously accelerates and is threatened by AI displacement.

H-1B approvals vs. tech layoffs, major firms

Firms simultaneously receiving H-1B approvals and announcing workforce reductions — a key tension in the current policy debate.

In FY2025, one software company, which is widely reported to be Microsoft (White House, Sep 2025), received over 5,000 H-1B approvals while simultaneously announcing 15,000-16,000 layoffs. Also, Amazon reduced its workforce by 27,000 since 2022 while approving 25,000+ H-1B workers over the same period. AI adoption is being used to justify domestic workforce reduction while immigration channels keep high-exposure roles filled at lower cost — compressing wages and narrowing entry points for both native-born and immigrant workers.

Comparative Case

South Korea: Anti-Immigration meets AI Vulnerability

South Korea presents a structural contrast to the U.S. model. With only ~3.9% of its workforce foreign-born (vs. 19.1% in the U.S.), Korea has historically maintained tight immigration controls. Yet the country faces compounding pressures: a rapidly aging population, the world's lowest fertility rate (TFR 0.75 in 2024), and a labor structure that the IMF identifies as highly polarized between AI-complementary professional roles and AI-exposed clerical ones.

Cross-National Comparison

Immigration Openness as an
AI-Vulnerability Modifier

In immigrant-heavy economies, AI exposure risk is distributed across a larger, more mobile workforce. In restrictive economies like Korea, the same AI disruption lands on a demographically shrinking, aging domestic base — with no immigrant buffer to absorb transition costs.

Chart E

Foreign-born workforce share vs. occupational AI exposure — selected countries (illustrative)

Countries with higher immigrant workforce shares tend to have greater flexibility in absorbing labor market disruption. Low-immigration economies face a double constraint: demographic decline and AI displacement hitting the same shrinking domestic workforce. Note: the country-level AI exposure values (0–100) are illustrative estimates from the synthesized index described in the methodology note — no single published dataset provides exact per-country indices on this scale.

The structural argument: In the U.S., when AI displaces a domestic software developer, the labor market adjusts partly through immigrant workers filling adjacent roles, transitioning into new AI-adjacent positions, or returning home — a form of involuntary labor market flexibility. In Korea, there is no such buffer. AI displacement of clerical workers hits a labor pool that is already shrinking due to aging, declining birth rates, and growing talent emigration — creating a structural convergence of AI disruption and demographic crisis that no single policy can address in isolation.

Research Implications

What This Means for Our Framework

Incorporating immigration policy as a variable transforms our analysis from a simple G7 occupational exposure comparison into a structural vulnerability framework. The key insight: AI exposure index alone is insufficient to predict real labor market harm. The same exposure score produces different outcomes depending on a country's demographic flexibility — and immigration is the primary mechanism of that flexibility in advanced economies.

Finding 01

High-immigration economies (U.S., Canada) distribute AI exposure risk across a more mobile, international workforce — partially buffering domestic employment shocks.

Finding 02

The H-1B system creates a paradox: immigrant labor fills AI-exposed roles at potentially lower cost, compressing wages — yet immigrant founders and engineers also build the AI systems driving displacement.

Finding 03

South Korea faces compounding structural risk: high AI exposure in clerical roles, zero immigration buffer, and accelerating demographic decline — a uniquely vulnerable profile with no near-term policy resolution.
